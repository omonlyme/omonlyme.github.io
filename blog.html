> cat 2026-05-12-ceh-exam-notes.md What the CEH Exam Doesn't Tell You 2026-05-12 6 min

I walked into the Certified Ethical Hacker exam expecting something close to a live CTF — ports to scan, shells to pop. It's closer to a very long, very dry vocabulary test with occasional scenario questions. That mismatch cost me more prep time than any actual topic did.

What actually mattered: knowing the exact phase names (reconnaissance, scanning, gaining access, maintaining access, covering tracks) cold, being able to match tools to phases without hesitating, and treating the practice exams as the real syllabus — the official courseware wanders, the question bank doesn't.

The certificate itself doesn't make you dangerous. It makes you legible to people who hire for security roles, which turns out to matter more than I expected going in.

> cat 2026-02-20-cyberbullying-detector.md Teaching a Model to Recognize Cyberbullying (and What Broke First) 2026-02-20 8 min

The pitch for this project was simple: flag abusive content before a human has to see it. The preprocessing was where all the actual difficulty lived. Hashtags and mentions carry meaning that naive tokenization destroys — strip them wrong and the model loses the exact signal you wanted it to learn from.

Sarcasm and reclaimed slurs broke the first version badly. A sentiment layer helped more than I expected, not because sentiment alone predicts abuse, but because it gave the classifier a second, differently-biased signal to reconcile against the raw text features.

The uncomfortable lesson: precision and recall pull against each other in exactly the cases that matter most — a model that's too aggressive silences real conversation, one that's too lenient defeats the point of building it at all.

> cat 2025-11-05-resume-parser-irony.md I Built a Resume Parser and Now I Trust Resumes Even Less 2025-11-05 5 min

Building an entity extractor for resumes means staring at hundreds of them in a row, and after enough repetitions the formatting patterns start to feel less like self-presentation and more like a genre with its own tropes — "spearheaded," "synergy," a skills section that's really a keyword-stuffing exercise for whatever ATS is reading it.

The technical side was its own puzzle: PDFs don't preserve document structure the way you'd hope, so "which line is the job title vs. the company name" ends up depending on layout heuristics almost as much as the actual NLP model.

Somewhere in that pile of PDFs I made peace with my own resume being a rough draft of who I'm becoming, not a deposition. The parser doesn't judge. Mostly.

> cat 2025-08-30-oceanarc-exim-lessons.md Shipping OceanArc Exim: Full-Stack Lessons From a Real Client 2025-08-30 7 min

Every side project I'd shipped before this had exactly one stakeholder: me. OceanArc Exim had a client who needed the inquiries form to actually reach their inbox reliably, which reframed half my "nice to have" backlog into "actually load-bearing."

The REST API design ended up simpler than my first draft — fewer, chunkier endpoints that matched how the frontend actually consumed data, instead of a purist one-resource- per-endpoint layout that looked better in a diagram than it worked in practice.

MongoDB's schema flexibility was a genuine advantage here: the content the client wanted to manage kept shifting shape in the first two weeks, and a rigid relational schema would have meant a migration every time they changed their mind.
